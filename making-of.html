Why Margo exists

Find the font you read fastest — then set it on your Kindle, in your browser, or anywhere you read. Research shows switching to your personal fastest font can add 30+ words per minute. That's hours of reading time per year.

Try Margo — it takes 5 minutes →

On a Friday morning in March, I came across a link on Hacker News — a PDF about typeface legibility. I didn't read it. I asked Claude to summarize it. That turned into a question about dark mode vs. light mode, which turned into a question about Inter — if it's really the GOAT — which turned into: "should we make a mini web app?"

By end of day, margo.fyi was live. I didn't write any code.

But the story isn't: "just ask Claude to make an app." I mean, you can try. Actually, definitely try. But it's been 13 years, or 20 if you count high school and college, since I started designing. I knew which questions to ask (I've asked them before), I knew which outputs to push back on, and when something felt off, I had Claude fix it.

That judgment is the work. The refining is the work. The bug fixing. Making things feel buttery smooth. That's the work. The tool removes the friction between thinking and making.

What follows is the full conversation — including the back-and-forth, the design debates, the bugs, the naming arguments (which, for me, always happen too early), and the moments where things got better because someone pushed back. Claude even called one of my suggestions "the most obvious pun in typography." I love it!

Have a read — hope it helps!

The research rabbit hole

It started with a PDF summary and turned into a deep dive on serif vs. sans serif, dark mode, and which fonts actually outperform in academic studies. About 40 minutes of research that would have taken days to do manually.

Ron
Can you summarize this: library.oapen.org/…/978-3-030-90984-0.pdf
Claudius
The link points to "The Legibility of Serif and Sans Serif Typefaces" by John T. E. Richardson (Springer, 2022). Richardson takes a rigorous, skeptical approach — the evidence for strong serif-vs-sans differences is much weaker and more conditional than commonly claimed. For screens: slight edge to sans serif. For print: slight edge to serif. Bottom line: serif for long-form print, sans for screens, and don't lose sleep over it.
Ron
so the bottom line in which one is easier/faster to read: "it depends"?
Claudius
Pretty much — but Richardson's main argument is less "it depends" and more "the confident claims on both sides are overblown." The actual measured differences in reading speed are often tiny or statistically unreliable. A lot of the design-world consensus is more tradition than hard science.
Ron
Is there other research re: dark mode vs light mode? Past bosses have asserted that light mode is easier to read. Likely true if you're judging for accessibility but I wonder what the data says.
Claudius
Your boss isn't wrong — but the full picture is more nuanced. In people with normal vision, visual performance tends to be better with light mode. The mechanism: a bright background causes the pupil to contract, which reduces spherical aberrations — your eye has to work less to focus. That said: long-term reading in light mode may be associated with myopia. Dark mode may reduce eye fatigue during extended use. The honest answer: offer both, default to light.
Ron
how about studies involving faster reading, or completion of task. does inter win? are other overlooked fonts top-performers?
Claudius
The landmark study: Adobe, Google, and UCF's Readability Consortium tested 16 fonts on 352 participants. Reading speeds increased by 35% comparing fastest vs. slowest fonts — without affecting comprehension. The surprising finding: people didn't know what their best font was. Preference didn't predict speed.

Inter doesn't appear in the formal academic studies — too new at the time — but its design credentials hold up. The underrated performers: Verdana (deeply uncool, genuinely excellent in studies), Noto Sans (specifically designed for small screens, wildly underused). The dark horse: Lexend — built from academic research, designed from the data rather than retrofitted to it.

The meta-finding: by simply changing to their personal fastest font, the average adult read an additional 30 words per minute — equivalent to 3–4 pages per hour.
Ron
should we consider making a mini web app that helps users find their font? It'd have them read different texts in a certain period of time, and ask them to identify the last word they read.
Claudius
Love this concept — it's basically a personal version of the UCF/Adobe study. The "last word you read" mechanic is clever: low-friction, naturally captures reading pace without requiring self-reporting. Before I build it, let me think through the design with you…
Ron
Q: How many fonts? A: can we get anything statistically valid from just 3? defaults to 5, user can choose 3, 5, or 7.
Q: Reading time per font? A: what's statistically valid? i'm guessing 30?
Q: How should the user mark their last word? A: Both options — click or type.
Claudius
On 3 fonts: directionally useful but fragile. 5 is the practical minimum for a recommendation you'd trust. 7 gives you a meaningful spread. 3 is fine labeled as "Quick Test (rough estimate)."

On 30 seconds: correct. Long enough for reading speed variance to emerge, short enough that fatigue doesn't become a confounding variable. ~200-250 WPM = ~100-125 words in 30s. That's a solid passage.

The bugs

Real software has real bugs. These three are worth studying — each one reveals something about how browsers actually work that isn't obvious from reading documentation.

Ron
clicking home doesn't pause the timer.
Claudius
Found the root cause. CSS transitions run on the compositor thread — completely separate from JavaScript. clearInterval stops the JS counter but the bar keeps sliding because it's a CSS animation, not a JS loop.

Fix: at the moment of pause, snapshot the bar's computed pixel width, convert to percentage, set that explicitly with transition: none to freeze it in place. On resume, use requestAnimationFrame to let the DOM settle before restarting the animation over exactly S.timerSecs remaining seconds.
This is a browser architecture issue, not a code mistake. The browser runs CSS transitions on the GPU for performance. Understanding this requires knowing how browsers render, not just how to write CSS. Most developers would reach for a JS-only timer animation — the better fix is to work with the CSS architecture rather than against it.
Ron
[uploaded screenshot showing text cut off, unable to scroll past it]

didn't seem to work. i can't scroll down further than this
Claudius
Classic flexbox scrolling bug. In a flex column, children have min-height: auto by default — the passage wrap won't shrink below its content height, so overflow never kicks in. Fix: min-height: 0.
Ron
nah that didn't fix it. there's something wrong with r-passage. the browser thinks its height is much shorter
Claudius
Found the real cause. The wrapper was display: flex with default align-items: stretch — which forces the inner passage to match the container's height rather than grow to its content height. The browser measured zero overflow and never triggered a scrollbar.

Fix: align-items: flex-start — lets the passage grow to its natural content height, which overflows the container and scrolls correctly.
The symptom (can't scroll) points nowhere near the cause (align-items: stretch collapsing the inner element). This is the kind of bug that takes 30 minutes to find if you don't know the flexbox model intimately. The first fix (min-height: 0) was a reasonable guess that addresses a different common flexbox scroll issue — but it wasn't this one.
Ron
Uncaught Error: Uncaught SyntaxError: Unexpected end of input
Claudius
[ran a Python brace-counting script on the extracted JavaScript]

Open braces: 130. Close braces: 129. Diff: 1. Found it — the doSkip function was missing its closing }. The function body was left open, silently swallowing every function definition after it until the parser hit end-of-file. That's why everything appeared to exist in the source but nothing ran.
The parser error — "Unexpected end of input" — points to the end of the file, not the actual location of the problem. The brace counter is the right tool: simple script, unambiguous output, found a needle in a 1500-line file in seconds.
